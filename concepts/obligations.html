Obligations

Imaging & UnconsciousUltimate ConsciousnessBeing & DoingIrreverent ActingNo Acting Please
The seven major responsibilities an actor must identify and fulfill when approaching dramatic material — the specific realities the character must experience as defined by the script. What the playwright and material demand of the actor. The actor uses choices and choice approaches to organically create each obligation from personal experience.

The seven main obligations: (1) Time and Place — the environment where the scene occurs; (2) Relationship — who the other characters are and how the character feels toward them; (3) Emotional — what the character feels; (4) Character — who the character is (physical, emotional, intellectual, psychological); (5) Historic — the historical period's customs, mores, and realities; (6) Thematic — the playwright's statement or message; (7) Subtextual — the unsaid, underlying emotional reality beneath the dialogue.

Morris contrasts obligations with Stanislavsky's 'objectives, superobjectives, actions, activities' — calling the latter counter-creative. Obligations defined in terms of the actor's task to create (not just play) allow the actor to use craft to organically stimulate the needed reality. The order of importance and approach varies with each piece of material; thorough reading of the material is prerequisite to identifying all obligations.

The Obligation-Choice-and-Choice-Approach Chart is the primary organizational tool for mapping this process. Three broader types exist in some formulations: instrumental obligations (relating to the actor's instrument), relational obligations (other people and objects), and role obligations (the character's requirements).
