Characterization

Ultimate ConsciousnessBeing & Doing
The actor's process of creating the life of a character not by 'becoming someone else' but by absorbing the character INTO oneself — using the actor's own multidimensional humanity as the instrument. Character elements are stimulated through choices and selective emphasis; the actor finds their own personal basis for each behavior. Versatility is a function of range of self-expression, not ability to pretend to be someone else.

Morris explicitly rejects the conventional notion of 'wearing the cloak of another person.' Morris even discourages the word 'character' because it implies separation from self. Everything onstage must come from the actor's own reality.

Four sources for understanding character: (1) what the author says about the character; (2) what other characters say to or about them; (3) what the character says about themselves; (4) the character's actions and interactions throughout the play.

Three choice approaches most recommended for characterization: Subpersonalities, Externals, and Affective Memory. The character is who the playwright says they are PLUS the unique individuality of the actor. When a play is cast, actors are usually chosen for similarities to the character — so the gap is often smaller than feared.
