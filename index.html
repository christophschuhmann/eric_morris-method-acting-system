The Eric Morris Acting System

Eric Morris is one of the most influential acting teachers in American theatre and film, whose students include Jack Nicholson, Arnold Schwarzenegger, and numerous other acclaimed performers. Over a career spanning more than fifty years and seven books, Morris developed a comprehensive acting system that builds on and extends the Stanislavsky-derived Method tradition brought to America by Lee Strasberg at the Actors Studio.

Where Strasberg's Method focuses on techniques like sense memory and affective memory to create emotional truth on stage, Morris identified a critical gap: these techniques only work if the actor's instrument is free enough to respond. Most actors carry deep-seated tensions, inhibitions, and emotional blocks from a lifetime of social conditioning — fear of judgment, need for approval, suppression of impulses. No amount of craft technique can produce genuine results through a blocked instrument. As Morris puts it: "You can't teach a man to run if he hasn't got legs."

Morris's solution was to develop an entirely new dimension of actor training — instrumental work — a systematic approach to freeing the actor's emotional instrument before applying craft. His system is built on the concept of BEING: the state of total, authentic presence in which the actor expresses everything felt moment-to-moment, without intellectual interference or social obligation. BEING is the canvas; technique is the paint. Morris argues that 90% of training should go to achieving BEING, with craft technique occupying the remaining 10%.

The complete system unfolds in three stages: Being → Irreverence → Ultimate Consciousness. First, the actor liberates the instrument and achieves genuine presence. Then, through irreverence — a courageous disregard for social and theatrical conventions — the actor learns to honor all impulses freely. Finally, in rare peak moments, the actor reaches Ultimate Consciousness, where unconscious impulses enrich conscious behavior, producing transcendent performance.

The System at a Glance

The problem Morris solves: Conventional actor training teaches craft techniques — sense memory, emotional recall, script analysis — but assumes the actor is emotionally available to use them. In reality, most actors are blocked by tension, social conditioning, fear, and ego problems that prevent genuine emotional access. They end up imposing behavior rather than experiencing it, producing what Morris calls "on the nose" acting: flat, one-dimensional performances that deliver the result of an emotion without the living process that created it.

How it works: The system operates on two tracks simultaneously. The instrumental track uses over a hundred exercises — from Personal Inventory and vulnerability work to tension-release techniques and ego-building preparations — to systematically dismantle the actor's emotional blocks and restore the natural stimulus → effect → response → expression cycle. The craft track provides a concrete methodology for approaching any piece of material: identify the seven obligations of the scene, select personal choices that can organically stimulate the required emotional life, and work for those choices through specific choice approaches like sense memory, endowments, imaginary monologues, and imaging.

What makes it unique: Unlike purely technical approaches (Meisner, Adler, Hagen) or purely psychological ones, Morris's system integrates the personal and the professional into a single, lifelong practice. The exercises are not just classroom tools — they are designed to become a way of life. The actor who practices daily Personal Inventory, sense memory, and BEING work develops an instrument that is perpetually ready, emotionally available, and capable of organic reality that audiences find irresistibly compelling. The craft, as Morris says, "is designed to do away with itself" — the more skilled the actor becomes, the less the technique shows, until only truth remains.
