230 conditional recommendations for actors

Actor wants to begin learning imaging

Start by becoming conscious of what imaging you already do naturally: what kinds of images, when they occur, what patterns emerge. Then practice Imaging with Sense Memory using real places or events from your life, always opening the eyes once the inner image is established.

Actor is visually dominant and neglects other senses in imaging

Deliberately begin Imaging Sensations exercises with a non-visual sense (smell, sound, or touch). Practice giving the non-visual senses the same total emphasis you'd give the visual. Over-correct until all senses become habitual.

Actor wants to use imaging as a choice approach in a scene

Identify the scene obligation, select a choice from your own life that would stimulate the appropriate emotional response, then create that choice through Imaging with Sense Memory. Work with all five senses simultaneously, become part of the image (do not watch it on a screen), and let the images run free between sensorial explorations.

Actor wants to access deeper, unconsciously rooted emotional life for a scene

During rehearsal, work for your conscious choice while carefully noting all involuntary images that arise. After the session, re-create those involuntary images using the Imaging technique — they likely represent the unconscious's own translation of your obligation and will produce richer emotional life than your original choice.

Actor uses Sense Memory alone and finds it overly analytical

Combine Sense Memory with Imaging: establish the overall environment through imaging first, then use sensorial questions to deepen specific elements. Allow the image to run freely between questions rather than remaining in pure question-response mode.

Use Fragmented Imaging with a personal theme (loss, love, childhood) that you know affects you. Let images tumble without needing them to connect. Steer toward the impacting moments using Selective Emphasis. Works much faster than Story Imaging.

Actor keeps anticipating and guiding his own choice exploration (self-facilitation bias)

Switch to Guided Imaging with a scene partner. Because you cannot predict the facilitator's questions, you explore the choice without premeditation — discovering what the choice actually does to you rather than what you expect it to do.

Scene partners have a long shared history that does not appear in the script

Use Shared Imaging to build that history. One actor shares images of relevant personal experiences; the other images exactly what is described (not parallels). Do this scene-by-scene, also covering extra-textual life between scenes. Consider doing sessions in a hypnagogic state to encourage the shared material to enter dreams.

Actor arrives at performance depressed, stressed, or emotionally shut down

Use Fantasy Story Imaging — find a topic that has always been pleasurably memorable (something that speeds up your heartbeat), then create a fully multi-sensory fantasy story based on it. Allow the emotional response to build and amplify. The clown/circus example demonstrates the transformation from depression to joy and love.

Actor has a persistent fear (auditions, exposure, ridicule, conflict) that keeps returning

Begin a daily imaging practice for fear elimination: (1) re-experience the fear's origins fully through sensory imaging until the source is clear; (2) change the image — rewrite the outcome to one of power, success, and confidence. Do this repeatedly over weeks or months. This reaches the unconscious where the fear was originally encoded and reprograms it.

Actor wants to use Verbalized Imaging but keeps describing images in words rather than experiencing them

Watch for the distinction: verbal description short-circuits the sensory process. When you verbalize, use minimal language as a trigger ('I see...', 'I smell...') and then let the sense respond sensorially. After verbalizing, reduce and eventually eliminate the words as the imaging becomes self-sustaining. Verbalize only to START the process and to stay on track.

Actor has difficulty starting the imaging process

Use Verbalized Imaging as a starting mechanism: begin by acknowledging the difficulty aloud ('I feel reluctant to start...I'm not sure where to begin...I'm afraid it won't work...'), then sneak into imaging by simply starting 'I see...' and filling in the first thing that comes. The acknowledgment of obstacles clears them so the imaging can begin.

Actor is blocked on the spot before a performance or class and cannot reach a BEING state

Use I'm Five Years Old imaging: start the prompt 'I'm five years old and I...' and respond with images, not words. Progress through ages. This simultaneously creates selfless involvement, elevates affectability, and piques unconscious memories — tripling the instrumental benefit in a single exercise.

Actor needs to build an authentic relationship with a scene partner quickly

Layer three approaches: (1) Shared Imaging — each shares personal experiences that parallel the play's circumstances; (2) Guided Imaging — facilitate each other through relevant personal experiences without anticipatory bias; (3) Relationship Imaging — while relating to the real partner, silently image modifications in their behavior. Progress to Endowment via Imaging once a foundation is established.

Actor wants to build the unconscious background of a character between rehearsals

Practice Parallel Imaging daily: image yourself living the character's life using the play's actual circumstances. Weave in personal parallels where they resemble the character's experiences. Do this before sleep or in the sleep-wake state for faster unconscious absorption. Keep a journal of which images produce the most impact.

Actor's imagination feels weak, overly literal, or unable to generate creative choices

Do daily imagination-elevating exercises: Object Imaging (give an ordinary object a complete life and story), Impulsive Object Imaging (allow bizarre, illogical images from ordinary objects), Free-Association Imaging, and Fantasy Imaging. When images become outrageous or nonsensical, celebrate — it means the imagination is hooking into the unconscious.

Actor is addressing the time and place obligation and the environment is unfamiliar

Select a personal place that carries the same emotional and historical meaning as the play's environment — not just visually similar. Consider the character's history in that place. Create it through Imaging with Sense Memory if it is remote; use Imaging Sensations if it is vivid. Put yourself in it; surround yourself with the sensory elements; let the emotional response come from the place itself.

A scene requires an emotional transition between two distinct states at a specific moment

Use Fragmented Imaging transitionally: establish the first emotional state with positive sensory fragments in preparation, then at the moment of shift, rapidly generate contrasting fragments (e.g., from bright eyes/laughter → sad eyes/stooped shoulders/gray skin). No words—only sensory flashes. The contrast drives the organic shift.
