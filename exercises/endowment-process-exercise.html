The Endowment Process (Sensory Exercise) Craft

Irreverent Acting
Goal

Transform the perceived qualities of the other actor or an object sensorially, so that the actor responds to a changed stimulus that fulfills the scene's obligations.

How To

Begin with the available actor or object as it really is. Ask sensory questions directed at the desired change, responding with the appropriate sense: eyes (color, clouding, blood vessels), skin (texture, color, temperature), hair (texture, hairline), posture (tremor, head position, shoulders), voice (sound, placement, word formation). For endowing another actor with a specific person's features: first identify similarities between the actress's face/body and the imaginary person's, then identify differences and sensorially begin to mold the actress's features toward the imaginary person. Continue asking hundreds of sensory questions across all sensory modalities until the imaginary person's features begin to overlay the real actor. As you succeed and become emotionally affected by the changes, the nature of the sensory questions changes organically.

Who Should Do It

Any actor who needs to feel a fundamentally different relationship to the scene partner than what the actual person provides; or who needs to relate to a prop or set element as something it isn't.

Why It Works

The Sense Memory process, when applied to an already-existing object or person, begins to shift the actor's perception. The conglomerate stimulus (real actor + sensory endowment) becomes the living embodiment of the choice.

Additional Info

One choice and one choice approach can address two obligations simultaneously (relationship + emotional). The process can go on as long as it takes to complete the transformation.
