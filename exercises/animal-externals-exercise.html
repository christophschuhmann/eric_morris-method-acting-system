Animal Externals (Approach Exercise) Craft

Irreverent Acting
Goal

Absorb the sense of a specific animal through the actor's own physical instrument, then translate that sense into human behavior for use in a role.

How To

Find a place where you can observe a specific animal for long periods (zoo, wildlife, video study). Step 1—Overall rhythm: watch and practice the animal's rhythm; take it home and repeat; return and refine. Step 2—Leading center: identify what part of the animal crosses an imaginary line first; apply to your own body. Step 3—Secondary center: find the part that closely follows the leading center; put all three together. Step 4—Limitations: study and add what the animal cannot do; adapt to your body. Step 5—Centers of weight, balance, and power: identify and add to exploration. Step 6—Isolations: practice independent movement of body parts; break animal down and reassemble. Step 7—Corresponding and contrasting rhythms: identify parts moving in harmony vs. counterrhythm; work separately, then together. Step 8—Species and individual mannerisms: species mannerisms first; then individual. Add tempo (speed of rhythm) once secure with rhythms. Translation to human behavior: from all-fours to crouching to standing, retaining all elements, making them progressively more subtle. Sounds: explore where sounds originate in the animal's body; reproduce them from the same body location.

Who Should Do It

Any actor confronting a character requiring physical attitudes, primal impulses, or behavioral qualities not accessible through other means.

Why It Works

Approaching inner life from the outside in piques the unconscious. The body leads, and the thoughts, impulses, and emotional life follow the physical instrument's transformation.

Additional Info

Time frame is entirely up to the actor. In public rehearsals, bring only the translated (human) form; in private work, use the full animal position. 'The work or process of a master craftsman is undetectable except to the eyes of an expert.'
