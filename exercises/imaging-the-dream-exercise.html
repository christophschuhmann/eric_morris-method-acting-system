Imaging the Dream Instrumental

Imaging & Unconscious
Goal

Re-create a vivid or recurring dream through Imaging to establish a conscious connection with the unconscious emotional material it contains, and optionally to use it as a preparation for a scene.

How To

1. Record the dream immediately upon awakening. 2. Choose the most impacting, vividly memorable dream (not all dreams need re-creation). 3. Begin with a relaxation preparation (when sleeping, you are relaxed—match that state). 4. Close your eyes and lie down if possible. 5. Begin imaging the dream from the beginning. Allow images to come up spontaneously. 6. Use all five senses; do not lead or premeditate the experience. 7. At moments of high emotional intensity, encourage and stay with the feelings— the greater the emotional involvement, the closer you are to piquing unconscious response. 8. Repeat the re-creation several times. 9. If using as acting preparation: after completing the dream imaging, move directly into working for a choice that addresses the scene's emotional obligations.

Who Should Do It

All actors seeking to deepen unconscious access. Particularly valuable for actors who have vivid dreams or recurring dreams that seem charged with emotional significance.

Why It Works

Dreams are the unconscious's primary communication medium. Re-creating a dream while awake brings the unconscious material into conscious experience. The dream does not need to be interpreted—the emotional experience of it IS the value. Recurring dreams are especially rich because they indicate the unconscious has a persistent agenda.

Additional Info

Morris's example: recurring dream of driving into a lake. Connected to childhood near-drowning in Franklin Park, Chicago, at age 12. Life flashing by before eyes during actual drowning event. The fear of water and falling has unconscious roots in this experience.
