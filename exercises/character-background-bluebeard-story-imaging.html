Character Background Story Imaging: Love from a Stranger (Bluebeard) Craft

Imaging & Unconscious
Goal

Demonstrate how to build the psychological history of a morally extreme character (serial killer) through a series of Story Imaging episodes, mixing real parallels with fantasy.

How To

1. Thoroughly read the script; note all facts about the character's history (however sparse). 2. Decide whether to use total fantasy or Believability (real + fantasy). Recommended: Believability. 3. Begin the first imaging episode at age five: 'I'm in my bedroom...I hear my parents arguing...' Use all five senses: peeling wallpaper, smell of the room, sounds through the wall, etc. 4. Let a significant emotional event occur in the image (mother's drunken rage, physical strike). Experience it with full sensory specificity. 5. End the episode, then move to the next developmental period (school years, adolescence, etc.). 6. Each episode should be infinitely more elaborate than indicated—all senses specifically engaged. 7. The actor uses real connections from their own life (e.g., actually was poor, felt unwanted) mixed with invented events consistent with the character profile.

Who Should Do It

Actors playing characters with extreme or alien psychology—villains, severely traumatized individuals, moral outcasts—where personal parallel experience is insufficient.

Why It Works

By constructing a coherent narrative of the character's formative experiences, the actor creates an emotional logic for all of the character's behavior in the play. The mix of real and invented material triggers unconscious responses and makes the history feel lived.

Additional Info

The story imaging must produce the specific emotional underpinnings the character needs: in this case, humiliation, rejection, and hatred of women as symbols of the mother. Multiple episodes (5-10+) should be created in chronological order.
