Four-Section Dream Journal Entry Both

Imaging & Unconscious
Goal

Create a complete, structured dream journal entry that transforms a raw dream into an analyzed, translated, and re-created acting resource.

How To

SECTION 1 – DESCRIBE: Write the entire dream as soon as you awaken. Record exactly: what happened, who was there, what they did, what you felt, in what order. Leave nothing out, even if it seems trivial. SECTION 2 – INTERPRET: Identify all symbols and symbolic behaviors. List all feelings and reactions—yours and others'. Determine: are the people subjective (parts of you) or objective (others in your life)? Use Active Imagination: put each key figure in a chair, ask them questions. Check for connections to your waking life. Check for disowned subpersonalities seeking recognition. SECTION 3 – TRANSLATE: List the emotional components and the parts of yourself the dream reveals. Identify acting choices: what material obligations could this dream address? Note which of its energy states could serve as subpersonality choices. SECTION 4 – RE-CREATE (choose one or more): A. Original perspective: re-create the dream as you dreamt it (Story/Fragmented Imaging). B. Alternative perspective: inhabit a different dream figure. C. Objective perspective: observe the dream as a detached witness. Write the results of each re-creation in the journal.

Who Should Do It

All actors doing dream work and Programming the Unconscious.

Why It Works

The structured approach prevents the actor from treating dreams as mere curiosities. Each section extracts a different type of value: description preserves the raw material; interpretation reveals the unconscious message; translation creates actionable acting tools; re-creation builds the conscious-unconscious bridge.

Additional Info

Marty's dream journal example: Description: fight behind barn, actor in hayloft watching. Interpretation: Witness State, killer/warrior energies in combat. Translation: access warrior energy for violent characters. Re-creation A: Fragmented Imaging from hayloft perspective—actor felt excited but uninvolved. Re-creation B: Assumed identity of one fighter—felt like killer AND victim; yelled and screamed.
