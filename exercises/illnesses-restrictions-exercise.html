Illnesses and Restrictions — Creating a Headache Craft

Irreverent Acting
Goal

Sensorially create a physical sensation (headache, bodily pain, or restriction) that organically produces the character behavior required by the scene.

How To

Direct sensory attention to the affected body part and ask sensory questions responded to in that area: What do I feel in my forehead?...How high above my eyebrows?...Where do I feel the most?...How far does that feeling reach?...The right side?...The left?...How high up the head?...Around the sides?...At the back?...As I slowly move my head, what do I experience?...When I close my eyes, what happens?...Where does the pain seem to come from?...How does it change as it moves away from that point?...As I move, what happens in my head?...How do the sounds in this room affect the feeling?...What sounds particularly affect the pain?...As I bend forward, backward, what happens? For restrictions (body brace, handcuffs, neck brace): ask questions about the presence of the object—where does it touch the body, what is the texture and temperature, how much movement is prevented, what does resistance feel like.

Who Should Do It

Any actor required to play illness, pain, physical restriction, or any emotional state that a physical restriction would organically produce (irritability, fear, panic, helplessness).

Why It Works

The base reality (real sensory experience of pain or restriction) produces all the organic behavior that results. The actor doesn't communicate pain—he experiences it, and the behavior follows authentically.

Additional Info

Works for any kind of physical restriction. A person in real pain behaves differently even in voice quality. The sensory process is the same as any Sense Memory work—the questions aim at the relevant body part.
